## 1. Comparable Niches Strategy

**When to use:** Default strategy. Works for any niche, any time.

**Core principle:** Look at channels in *adjacent* niches (not direct competitors) and adapt their proven ideas. This gives you ideas that are new to your audience but have already proven to work in a similar content style.

**Why adjacent, not competitors?** Taking from competitors means your audience has likely already seen it. Taking from a comparable niche means the idea is fresh to your audience, but it's still proven to work in the same *type* of content (education, entertainment, etc.).

### How to Execute

1. **Identify comparable channels.** If you're an education creator, look at other educators in different topics. If you're in business, look at self-improvement. If you're in fitness, look at nutrition. The key: same content *style*, different subject matter.

2. **Sort by their top-performing recent videos.** Focus on videos from the last 6-12 months. Older videos may not reflect current audience behavior.

3. **For each title, adapt it to your niche.** Do a direct translation:
   - "Deep Focus: Why Your Brain Needs Under-stimulation" → "YouTube Growth: Why You Need Over-stimulation" or "Why YouTube Isn't Luck"
   - "How I Read Hard Books Really Fast" → "How to Blow Up on YouTube Really Fast"
   - "The Disturbing Psychology of Friendships" → "The Disturbing Psychology of YouTubers"
   - "How to Quickly Understand Difficult Things" → "How to Quickly Understand the YouTube Algorithm"

4. **Don't try to make each one perfect.** Just riff fast. Put down even bad ideas — they might spark good ones later. The goal is VOLUME.

5. **Aim for at least 10-15 adapted ideas per comparable channel.** Most will be mediocre. That's fine. You're looking for the 1-2 that make you go "oh damn."

### Example Comparable Channels by Niche
- **YouTube Growth:** Elizabeth Phillips (study/education), Ali Abdaal (productivity), thought2 (faceless/entertainment)
- **Business/Finance:** Self-improvement creators, career coaches, tech reviewers
- **Fitness:** Nutrition channels, mental health, biohacking
- **Entertainment:** Other entertainment verticals (pranks → challenges, vlogs → documentary)

## 2. Viewer Searches Strategy

**When to use:** When you have access to YouTube Studio analytics or know what the audience is searching for.

**Core principle:** Your audience is literally telling you what they want. Their search queries are direct windows into their desires, pain points, and curiosity. Build ideas that answer those exact queries.

### How to Execute

1. **Go to YouTube Studio → Research tab → Viewer Searches.** (Or use what you know about common searches in the niche.)

2. **For each search query, come up with 2-3 video ideas:**
   - "video editing" → "How to Edit Better Than 99% of YouTubers" / "I Edited a Viral Video to Prove It's Not Luck"
   - "how to make money online" → "The Secret to Making Money on YouTube" / "How I Made $100,000 on YouTube"

3. **Look for high-volume, emotionally charged searches.** These indicate strong desire, which means easier clicks.

4. **Try to make some ideas sound impossible.** "I Edited a YouTube Video Without a Computer" — wild, but attention-grabbing.

## 3. Popular Analysis (Interest Extraction)

**When to use:** When you want to develop genuinely *new* ideas, not adaptations. This is the most advanced strategy.

**Core principle:** Instead of copying a competitor's title, you extract the underlying *interest* (what the audience actually wants from that video) and then build completely original ideas that serve that same interest.

### How to Execute

1. **Go to a competitor channel. Look at their top-performing recent videos.**

2. **For each video, write down the audience desire — not the topic, but the *want*:**
   - "Editing Hacks YouTubers Use to Hook You" → Desire: "I want to edit a good video that hooks the viewer"
   - "If I Started a New YouTube Channel I'd Do This First" → Desire: "I want to start a YouTube channel the right way"
   - "I Blew Up a YouTube Channel as Fast as I Could" → Desire: "I want to blow up fast on YouTube"

3. **Delete the original titles. Work only from the desires.**

4. **For each desire, come up with 10 completely new video concepts:**
   - "I want to edit so good the viewer can't leave" → "How to Edit So Good the Viewer Can't Leave"
   - "I want to start the right way" → "How to Start a YouTube Channel with 100% Success"
   - "I want to blow up fast" → "How to Blow Up a YouTube Channel in 90 Days"

5. **This produces genuinely original ideas** that tap into proven desires. The ideas are new, but the underlying audience psychology is validated.

## 4. Outlier Research

**When to use:** When you want data-backed ideas that are virtually guaranteed to perform.

**Core principle:** Find videos that massively outperformed their channel's average (5x+). These represent ideas that resonated far beyond the creator's existing audience — meaning the idea itself has exceptional pull.

### How to Execute

1. **Use tools like 1of10.com or similar outlier-detection tools.** Search for your niche keyword.

2. **Look for videos with:**
   - 5x+ their channel's average views (minimum threshold)
   - High views relative to low subscriber count (indicates the idea pulled in non-subscribers)
   - Recent (within last 12 months preferred)

3. **For each outlier, ask:**
   - Why did THIS video break out? What's the hook?
   - Can I adapt this concept to my niche?
   - What's the underlying desire it's tapping into?

4. **Also look at the inverse:** Channels with high subscriber counts where ONE video massively outperformed. The idea did the heavy lifting, not the audience.

### Red Flags to Ignore
- Videos older than 2 years (audience behavior has changed)
- Trailers or announcements (artificially boosted)
- Trend-based videos (one-time spikes, not repeatable)

## 5. AI-Assisted Brainstorming

**When to use:** As a supplement to other strategies. Good for getting unstuck or finding angles you wouldn't have thought of.

**Core principle:** AI is not great at generating perfect ideas from scratch, but it excels at generating raw concept volume that you can then refine. Use it as a creative sparring partner, not a replacement for your judgment.

### How to Execute

1. **Start broad:** "Come up with 10 video ideas about [niche] for [audience]"

2. **Most will be mediocre.** That's expected. Look for interesting *concepts* or *keywords*, not finished titles.

3. **When you spot something interesting, drill down:** "Come up with 10 more titles based on [that interesting concept]"

4. **Feed it your existing ideas:** "Here are my 10 best video ideas. Come up with 10 more based on these." The more context AI has, the better the output.

5. **Use it for title variations:** "Come up with 10 different ways to say [this title]" — especially useful for the bulk title-writing phase.

6. **Look for unexpected combinations.** AI sometimes produces weird mashups that spark genuinely creative ideas. "The Copy-and-Paste Art of Thumbnail Creation" came from AI suggesting "art of thumbnail creation" and the creator adding the contradiction of "copy-and-paste."

### What AI Does Well
- Generating high volume of raw concepts quickly
- Finding new angles on established topics
- Creating title variations with different word choices
- Surfacing keywords and phrases you wouldn't have considered

### What AI Does Poorly
- Judging what will actually get clicks (no taste)
- Understanding current YouTube trends and audience behavior
- Producing ready-to-use titles (they almost always need refinement)
- Knowing your specific audience's psychology

## 6. Random Word Generator

**When to use:** When you're completely stuck, or when you want truly wild/unexpected ideas. Used by MrBeast and Airrack.

**Core principle:** Force your brain to make creative connections it never would have made naturally. A random word acts as a creative constraint that pushes you into unexpected territory.

### How to Execute

1. **Go to a random word generator website.** Set it to one word at a time, all word types.

2. **For each word, force yourself to come up with at least ONE video idea.** No matter how bad it is. No skipping.
   - "Murder" → "YouTube is About to Murder All of Its Users"
   - "Photography" → "How to Start Photography as a Beginner"
   - "Chip" → "How to Make YouTube Videos More Delicious Than a Bag of Chips"
   - "Opposition" → "Grow on YouTube and Beat All of Your Opposition"
   - "Gas" → "Pour Gas on Your YouTube Videos and Set Them on Fire"
   - "Freshman" → "I Challenged 100 Freshmen to Dress Up Like Seniors"

3. **Go fast.** Don't overthink. The crazier the better. You can filter later.

4. **This works especially well for entertainment channels** where wild concepts are the currency. For education, it can still spark unexpected angles.

5. **Do at least 20-30 words in a session.** You might get 1-2 genuinely usable ideas, which is a great hit rate for the time invested.

## Combining Strategies

The best ideation sessions use multiple strategies. A recommended session:

1. Start with **Comparable Niches** (15 min) — gets the creative juices flowing with proven concepts
2. Do **Popular Analysis** on 2-3 competitor videos (15 min) — goes deeper into audience psychology
3. Check **Outlier Research** for data-backed winners (10 min) — adds evidence to your instincts
4. Run through **Random Word Generator** for 10 words (5 min) — shakes loose unexpected ideas
5. Take your top 20 raw ideas and **filter ruthlessly** using the 5-question framework

This process should generate 50-100 raw ideas, which filter down to 5-10 strong concepts, which filter down to 2-3 "hell yes" ideas ready for title writing and packaging.
