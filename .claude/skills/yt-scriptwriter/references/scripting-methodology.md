# The Linden Chasteen YouTube Scripting Methodology

This is the complete scriptwriting methodology. It covers every technique from hooks through CTAs, with the reasoning behind each one. Read this before writing any script.

---

## 1. HOOKS — Confirming the Click

The hook is the first 1-3 lines of your video. Its only job is to confirm to the viewer that they're in the right place — that this video is what the title and thumbnail promised.

### The Core Principle

The reason someone is watching your video is because of the title and thumbnail. They clicked on a promise. The best way to keep them watching is to immediately confirm that promise. Repeat the title and thumbnail — both in what you say (auditory) and what they see (visual).

### How It Works

The hook should essentially be a slight variation of the title, delivered in the first 1-3 seconds, with visuals that match the thumbnail.

**Example — Preston Plays:** Title: "My Investigation of the $2.3 Billion Mega Sphere." Opening frame: literally the same image as the thumbnail. First line: "This sphere cost $2.3 billion and I'm going to find out why." He's confirmed both the title AND thumbnail in the first 3 seconds. Instant feedback loop — the viewer clicked on that promise and immediately got it.

**Example — Linden's own video:** Title: "YouTube is a Video Game — Here's How You Win." First line: "YouTube is a video game, and if you understand how to play, you can level up fast and get millions of views, just like one of my clients who got..." Visual: YouTube logo rising from the bottom, matching the thumbnail. Direct copy of title/thumbnail in the first second.

### Rules for Writing Hooks

- Include the title (or a close variation) in the first 1-3 lines
- Match the thumbnail visually in the opening frame or first few seconds
- Don't make it the *exact* title — use a slight variation so it sounds natural
- The hook is both AUDITORY (what you say) and VISUAL (what they see on screen)
- Never open with something unrelated to the title/thumbnail — you'll lose them immediately
- Keep it short — the hook is not an intro. It's 1-3 sentences max.

### How to Draft a Hook

1. Write out your title
2. Rephrase it slightly as a spoken sentence
3. Plan a visual that mirrors the thumbnail (b-roll, animation, screen recording, on-camera setup)
4. Combine auditory + visual into the first 3 seconds

## 2. THE INTRO FRAMEWORK — Keeping Viewers Past 30 Seconds

The intro is everything from the hook through roughly the 30-60 second mark. This is where most viewers drop off, so it's critical. The framework below (adapted from Colin & Samir with additions) is designed to lock people in past that danger zone.

### The 3 Pre-Script Questions

Before writing the intro (or any part of the script), answer these three questions:

1. **How can I make this appeal to someone with 500 subscribers?** Beginners are the biggest audience. Give them frameworks and examples they can use immediately. If a beginner sees your content and thinks "I can actually do this," you've won.

2. **How can I make this appeal to someone with 10 million subscribers?** Advanced creators need new formats, angles, or data they haven't seen. Even experts benefit from fresh title frameworks or unconventional strategies.

3. **How can I make this appeal to my mom?** Someone who knows nothing about your topic. This forces you to find the universal human interest — data, stories, emotion, curiosity — that makes any topic compelling to anyone.

These three questions shape the entire script. They determine which examples to use, how to frame the value, and what universal hooks to lean on.

### The 5-Beat Intro Structure

**Beat 1: Confirm the title/thumbnail (we already did this in the hook)**

Reiterate what the video is about. "I wrote 7,500 titles to beat the YouTube algorithm." This is the hook, transitioning into the intro. Viewer knows they're in the right place.

**Beat 2: Social proof**

Establish that you actually know what you're talking about. This is critical, especially on smaller channels. Drop credibility early and often — aim for 2-3 mentions of social proof throughout the intro.

Social proof can be: personal results ("doing this has taken my channel from 0 to 5,000 subscribers in 2 months"), client results ("the same framework I used to take Joel Kaplan from 6,000 to 237,000 subscribers"), name-drops of people you've worked with, or any other concrete evidence of authority.

Everyone has social proof — you just need to find it. Personal life wins, gym achievements, a short that blew up to 100K views — anything that establishes you have authority on this topic. Squeeze it in.

**Beat 3: Open loops / new narratives**

Raise questions that you don't answer yet. This plays on the psychological principle that humans feel uncomfortable with incompleteness — once a question is raised, they need the answer.

Example: "99% of small YouTubers are writing titles wrong, and it's likely the reason you're still waking up with no new subscribers." This raises the question: "What's the right way?" without answering it. The viewer now has an open loop they need closed.

You can stack multiple open loops: "But there is a step-by-step framework that the top YouTubers use to write titles, and once you learn it, your videos will go from this to this." Now there are TWO open questions: what is the framework, and what does "this to this" look like?

**Beat 4: Set expectations + more credibility**

Tell the viewer what they're going to get from this video. Combine it with another line of social proof.

Example: "And this is the same framework that I used to take Joel Kaplan from 6,000 to over 237,000 subscribers." This sets the expectation (you'll learn this framework) AND adds more social proof (it's proven with a real result).

**Beat 5: Establish singularity**

Why is YOUR video unique? What makes your perspective different from the 500 other videos on this topic? This is often tied to your mission or personal story.

Example: "But today I'm giving it all away for free, because I'm on a mission to take 1,000 people to over 100,000 subscribers." That's singularity — a unique POV and mission that no other video on this topic can claim.

## 3. STORY STRUCTURES — The Architecture of Retention

Even educational and tutorial videos need story structure. Without it, the viewer gets bored. Story is what takes someone from one point to another without losing them in the middle.

### Three-Act Structure

This is the dominant structure for entertainment and challenge content. MrBeast, Airrack, and most top entertainment creators use it. It's also valuable for educational content as a macro-level framework.

**Act 1 — Setup/Introduction:** Introduce the story, the characters, and the stakes. Set up new narratives and foreshadow what's coming. By the end of Act 1, there should be a plot point that launches the viewer into the meat of the video.

**Act 2 — Confrontation/Rising Action:** This is the bulk of the video. The stakes keep getting higher. Challenges escalate. Information builds. The viewer is being pulled toward the climax through progressively more intense content. The cuts get faster, the energy builds, the tension ratchets up.

**Act 3 — Climax + Falling Action + Resolution:** The climax is the highest point of tension or emotion in the entire video. This is what the whole video has been building toward. After the climax, the falling action settles everything — the emotion comes down, the story resolves, and the viewer feels satisfied.

**MrBeast Example:** In the "I built a giant death trap" video — Act 1 introduces Mac and the challenges, Act 2 builds through escalating stakes ($100K, $200K, aerial challenge), the climax is the devastating cookie-break moment at $700K, and the falling action is Mac's emotional aftermath. That build → climax → release is what creates a truly satisfying viewer experience.

The goal is to manufacture emotion. If you can make someone feel something — heart pumping, jaw dropping, laughing, crying — they will never click off your video and they'll never forget it.

### Hero's Journey

The Hero's Journey is the structure behind virtually every great adventure story, Disney movie, and many viral videos/shorts. It follows this progression:

1. **Status Quo** — Character in their ordinary life
2. **Call to Adventure** — Something disrupts the ordinary and calls them to action
3. **Assistance** — Someone or something helps them prepare
4. **Departure** — They leave for the journey
5. **Trials** — They face challenges and overcome obstacles
6. **Approach** — They gear up for the big challenge
7. **Crisis / Climax** — They face the "big boss" or ultimate challenge
8. **Treasure / Result** — They achieve their goal
9. **Return** — They journey back or transition to aftermath
10. **New Life** — They show how the experience changed them
11. **Resolution** — Everything is tied up and resolved

**MrBeast Short Example (984M views):** "Would you fly to Paris for a baguette?" Status quo (at Duke University) → Call to Adventure (MrBeast asks the question) → Assistance (flight included, getting ready) → Departure (flying to Paris) → Trials (finding the baguette shop) → Climax (enjoying Paris) → Treasure (the baguette) → Return (flying home) → Resolution (giving it to MrBeast). Every single beat is hit, which is a major reason it went so viral.

### When to Use Which

- **Three-Act**: Entertainment, challenges, competition, any video where tension builds to a peak
- **Hero's Journey**: Transformation stories, adventure content, "I did X" videos, travel
- **Both combined**: The highest-level content uses elements of both — most movies and the best YouTube videos use a Hero's Journey *within* a Three-Act framework

These rules are meant to be broken once you've mastered them. But until then, follow them as closely as possible — they give you a proven framework instead of guessing.

## 4. STORYTELLING CONNECTIVE TISSUE — The River Rocks Principle

This concept (from Nathaniel Drew and Johnny Harris) is the most fundamental storytelling principle: every point in your script must connect to the one before it.

### The Analogy

Think of your video as a river. The viewer is hopping across on rocks (scenes/points). If one rock is missing, they fall in and get swept away — they're lost, confused, and clicking off.

Your job is to make sure every rock is in place and close enough that the viewer can reach the next one.

### What This Means in Practice

Each line in your script should logically follow from the previous line. If you write "I'm going to the museum" and then "I'm at the pizza store," the viewer is lost — what happened in between? But if you write "I went to the museum, looked at art, got hungry, decided I wanted pizza, and went to the pizza store" — that flows. Every dot is connected.

**The Critical Rule:** Even if you miss ONE piece of context, the viewer is lost. Even if the rest of the video is perfect, once they're "in the river," they're trying to catch up instead of enjoying the content. Their mind is stuck on "wait, what just happened?" instead of absorbing your value.

### Non-Linear Storytelling

Connecting the dots doesn't mean your story has to be linear (1 → 2 → 3 → 4 → 5). You CAN use flashbacks, jump forward in time, or cut between timelines. But every jump needs a reason and a bridge. The connections still need to be there — the viewer just follows them in a different order.

### Application to Scripting

When writing your script, read each line after the previous one and ask: "Does this make sense? Would someone who just heard the last line understand why I'm saying this?" If the answer is no, add the connective tissue. Bridge the gap. Add the missing rock.

## 5. OPEN LOOPS — Manufacturing the Need to Keep Watching

Open loops are the single most powerful retention tool in your scriptwriting arsenal. They come from direct response copywriting, not YouTube — which is why most YouTubers don't use them effectively.

### The Psychology

When someone encounters an unanswered question, they feel psychologically uncomfortable. The cycle is: **Question → Incompleteness → Answer → Satisfaction.** Humans naturally don't want to be left in a state of incompleteness — they need the answer. This is so powerful that people will literally pull out their phones to Google something they don't know.

You can use this exact psychology in your scripts.

### How to Use Open Loops

Raise questions throughout your script by telling the viewer they're going to learn something — and then NOT giving them the answer yet.

**Example:** "There is one secret that I learned to increase my AVD, and it's something that no YouTuber has ever told you about." That raises the question "What is this secret?" AND confirms they don't already know it. The viewer now feels incomplete and MUST keep watching.

**Listicle technique:** "There are seven secrets to success." Until the viewer has heard all seven, they feel incomplete. Counting backwards (7 → 1) implies #1 is the most important, making it even harder to leave before the end.

### The Open Loop / Payoff Cycle

Your script should follow this pattern throughout the body:

```
Payoff 1 → Open Loop → Payoff 2 → Open Loop → Payoff 3 → Open Loop → ...
```

This is so important that it should literally be written into your outline template. Every time you close a loop (deliver a payoff), immediately open a new one. The viewer should NEVER have all their questions answered until the very end.

If they have all the information and no reason to stick around, they will leave.

## 6. PAYOFFS & THE EMOTIONAL ROLLERCOASTER

Payoffs are where you close loops and deliver value. But the way you deliver them matters just as much as what you deliver.

### The Highs and Lows

Your script should read like a rollercoaster — emotional lows followed by highs, over and over, building toward the biggest emotional moment at the climax.

**The Lows (taking the viewer down):** Touch on the viewer's struggles. Make it personal. Make it REAL — not some generic "it's hard out there" but genuine internal emotion that you and your audience have both felt.

Example of a LOW: "I remember waking up, looking at my phone, and seeing zero. Zero new subscribers. My video got five views. And that's one of the worst feelings in the world — feeling like you're never going to make it as a YouTuber because your growth is so slow it's going to take you a hundred years to get to 1,000 subscribers. That's a really disheartening and unmotivating feeling."

That's going DEEP into a genuine internal emotion. It can't be disingenuous — it has to be a real feeling that you and your audience both share.

**The Highs (pulling the viewer up):** After taking them low, you MUST pull them back up. Nobody wants to just watch a video where they get sad.

Example of a HIGH: "But then I started doing this one thing, and once I did, I started getting five subscribers a day. Then ten. Then fifteen. Now I'm at 5,000 subscribers, getting a couple hundred every single day, hundreds of thousands of views, and I did it all without spending a ton of time. Now YouTube makes so much sense and I'm able to make a full-time income online."

### The Pattern

```
Low → High → Explain → Low → High → Explain → ... → BIGGEST Low → BIGGEST High (climax)
```

The overall trajectory should be an upward trend, but with dips. And the highest high and lowest low should be at the climax — that's what makes the climax feel powerful.

Just like that MrBeast video: insane buildup (highs getting higher) → devastating loss (deepest low at the climax) → emotional aftermath (resolution). That swing from the highest high to the lowest low at the climax is what made viewers feel something real.

## 7. ASSUMPTIVE QUESTIONS — The Flow Hack

This technique comes from Alan Sulanic in the direct response copywriting world. It's one of the most powerful yet least-known techniques for making scripts flow better and retain longer.

### The Problem with Questions

When you ask a viewer a question, they have to think about it. That takes brain power. And most people on YouTube don't want to use brain power — they want to sit back and have the answers laid out for them. Every question creates friction. Friction causes drop-off.

### The Technique

1. **In your outline**, write out ALL the questions that need to be answered for the section to make sense to the viewer.
2. **Answer each question** with a paragraph or a few sentences.
3. **Delete the question** — just leave the answer.

The answers still work 99% of the time without the questions. The script flows better, there's less friction, and the viewing experience is more comfortable and relaxing for the audience.

### Example

**With questions (worse):**
"I like cake. But why does it have so much sugar? It's because America wants us to gain weight. But why does America want us to gain weight? It's because it's good for big companies. How is it good for big companies? Well, they make massive profits from it."

**Without questions — assumptive (better):**
"I like cake, but America wants us to gain weight, so they add crazy amounts of sugar to trick our dopamine receptors into addiction. Then we pay companies massive amounts to feed us poison."

The second version answers every question from the first version but without asking ANY of them. It's more intriguing, flows better, and creates less friction.

### The Process in Practice

When outlining a section about "title and thumbnail":

1. Write the questions: "How do I write a good title?" "Why do I need a good title?" "What will it do for my channel?"
2. Answer them: "Investing time into a good title and thumbnail increases your CTR from 3% to 10%, tripling the views on your video."
3. Delete the questions — just use the answers.

There ARE times when asking a question works (especially in open loops). But for the bulk of your script body, deleting questions and keeping only answers makes the script dramatically better.

## 8. THE CTA FRAMEWORK — Link, Curiosity Gap, Promise

This framework (credit: George Blackman) is how you get viewers to click on your next video. It's a 3-part formula that has been shown to increase end-screen click-through rates from ~2-3% to 8%+ — which on a video with 100K views means 5,000+ extra views on your next video just from this technique.

### Part 1: LINK

Connect the CTA back to what you were just talking about in the video. Don't just randomly drop a "check out this video" — bridge it naturally from the current topic.

Example: If you were talking about how important it is to get above 5% CTR, your link would be: "But to get above 5%, you need to have an eye-catching thumbnail..."

This gives the viewer a REASON to care about the next video — it's a natural extension of what they just learned.

### Part 2: CURIOSITY GAP

Open one final loop. Make the viewer feel incomplete about something related to the next video.

Example: "...and there's something that I've started doing recently that's made a huge impact on my click-through rate."

What has he started doing? The viewer doesn't know, and now they need to find out. That's the curiosity gap — an open loop that can only be closed by clicking the next video.

### Part 3: CTA + PROMISE

Tell the viewer what to do AND what they'll get if they do it.

Example: "So check out this video where I show you what it is and how you can use it to make your next thumbnail more viral than ever."

The CTA is "check out this video." The promise is "I'll show you what it is AND how to use it to make your thumbnail more viral." The viewer now has both the action and the reward.

### Full CTA Example

"In fact, Iman's creative director also told me that they monitor all of their old videos' click-through rates, and if one ever drops below 5%, they'll make a new thumbnail and A/B test it until they get back above five. **[LINK]** But to get above 5%, you need to have an eye-catching thumbnail, **[CURIOSITY GAP]** and there's something that I've started doing recently that's made a huge impact on my click-through rate. **[CTA + PROMISE]** So check out this video where I show you what it is and how you can use it to make your next thumbnail more viral than ever."

Always use the end-screen card and point to it. This framework has been proven to dramatically increase click-through to the next video.
