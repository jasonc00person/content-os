# Competitor Patterns

## Instagram

Nick Saraev's current winners are resource-led Claude/tool tutorials. The repeatable pattern is:

1. Comment-keyword CTA in the first line.
2. Named-tool stack in the hook.
3. Simple explanation of the problem.
4. Step-by-step setup.
5. Promise of the guide/template in DMs.

Chase's strongest comments come from "agentic OS" / Claude guide language. The view ceiling is lower than Nick, but comment rates can spike when the resource feels scarce or technical.

Mino does not lean as hard on comment bait. His best signal is curiosity and build-in-public credibility: concrete systems, student dashboards, roadmap systems, content-to-email automations.

BuiltByReid proves that universal beginner topics can crush niche AI content on raw view ceiling. The lesson is not "make finance content"; it is "translate complex stuff without making the viewer feel dumb."

## YouTube

Recent YouTube search is developer-heavy. Claude Code tutorials from Kevin Stratvert, Tech With Tim, Greg Isenberg, and AI Master rank because of authority, recency, and beginner framing.

Mino's long-form winners are process-as-spectacle:

- "watch me make a viral reel in 1 hour"
- "if I were starting a personal brand in 2026"
- "can I make a viral Instagram Reel in 1 HOUR?"

These work because they turn education into a watchable event.

## What To Steal

- Comment-keyword CTA mechanics.
- Tool-stack specificity.
- Screen-recorded proof.
- Time-bound challenge framing.
- "Beginner without big words" simplification.
- System demos where the viewer can see the output.

## What Not To Steal

- Generic dev tutorial posture.
- Robotic listicle scripts.
- Tool hype without tying it to content growth or revenue.
- Separate knowledge systems that do not feed the content pipeline.
